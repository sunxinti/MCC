**2. Analysis of Clinical Features**  
- **Delayed Puberty**: A 17-year-old with no breast development or menarche indicates primary hypogonadism. The normal external genitalia suggest intact müllerian structures, which typically develop under estrogen influence.  
- **Normal BMI and Height**: Rules out conditions like Turner syndrome (short stature) or severe malnutrition.  
- **Elevated Blood Pressure (BP)**: While not directly explained by primary hypogonadism, this could point to an additional endocrine abnormality.  
- **Normal Internal Genitalia**: The presence of normal female internal genitalia (uterus, fallopian tubes) implies that müllerian structures were not disrupted during embryogenesis, which requires the absence of anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH).  

**3. Differential Diagnosis Analysis**  
1. **Aromatase enzyme deficiency**:  
   - Aromatase converts androgens to estrogens. Deficiency would lead to low estrogen and high androgens, causing underdeveloped breasts but potentially virilizing features (e.g., hirsutism). However, internal genitalia would regress due to estrogen deficiency, conflicting with the case’s description of normal internal genitalia. **Unlikely**.  

2. **Hypokalemia**:  
   - Low potassium is seen in hyperaldosteronism or Cushing’s syndrome. While hypertension may occur, the patient is asymptomatic (no weakness, cramps), and there is no clear evidence of electrolyte disturbance. **Less likely**.  

3. **Increased levels of sex hormones**:  
   - Delayed puberty suggests low sex hormones, so this contradicts the clinical presentation. **Excluded**.  

4. **XY karyotype**:  
   - This is characteristic of **Swyer syndrome (46,XY pure gonadal dysgenesis)**. In this condition, the gonads are streak (non-functional), leading to absent estrogen production and delayed puberty. Despite an XY karyotype, normal female external genitalia develop due to lack of testosterone, and müllerian structures (uterus) persist because AMH is not produced (streak gonads cannot secrete AMH). The elevated BP may be coincidental or due to other factors unrelated to puberty delay. **Most likely**.  

5. **Hypercortisolism**:  
   - Excess cortisol suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, causing delayed puberty. Hypertension aligns with this, but Cushing’s typically presents with central obesity, moon face, or striae, which are absent here. A normal BMI makes this less likely unless due to an adrenal tumor (e.g., cortisol-secreting adrenal adenoma). However, Swyer syndrome better explains the normal genitalia and lack of other features. **Less likely than option 4**.  

**Final Selection**  
The patient’s normal internal genitalia rule out aromatase deficiency. The XY karyotype explains the absence of functional gonads (leading to estrogen deficiency and delayed puberty) while preserving female genitalia and müllerian structures. Elevated BP might be unrelated or due to another cause but does not negate the likelihood of Swyer syndrome.
